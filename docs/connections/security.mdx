Your database credentials are sensitive, and Conar is designed to handle them with care. Every connection string is encrypted before it ever leaves your device, and the sync mode you choose gives you direct control over whether your password is stored in the cloud at all. This page explains exactly how Conar protects your credentials and how to choose the right sync mode for each connection.

## Encryption at Rest

All connection strings stored by Conar — regardless of sync type — are encrypted at rest using a **per-user secret key**. This key is unique to your account and is never shared with other users. Even if Conar's storage were somehow accessed without authorization, your encrypted connection strings would be unreadable without your personal key.

Encryption is applied automatically every time you save a connection. You don't need to configure anything to benefit from it.

## Cloud (with password) vs Cloud (without password)

Conar gives you two sync modes, each offering a different trade-off between convenience and credential exposure. Both modes sync your connection across all your devices.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Cloud (with password)">
    **Cloud (with password)** stores your full connection string — including the password — in Conar's cloud, encrypted with your per-user secret key.

    - Your encrypted credentials are synced to every device where you sign in to Conar.
    - You never need to re-enter your password when switching devices or reinstalling the app.
    - The password is stored encrypted; Conar does not have access to the plaintext value.

    This option is convenient for development and staging databases where the priority is quick access across multiple machines.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Cloud (without password)">
    **Cloud (without password)** stores only connection metadata — hostname, port, database name, and username — in Conar's cloud. Your password is **never stored**.

    - Each time you open the connection, Conar prompts you to enter the password for that session.
    - The password is used in memory for the duration of your session and then discarded.
    - Even if your Conar account were compromised, an attacker would have no stored password to extract.

    This option is the right choice for production databases or any situation where your organization's security policy prohibits storing passwords in external systems.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Choosing the Right Sync Mode

Not sure which sync mode to use? Here's a straightforward guide:

| Situation | Recommended Sync Mode |
|---|---|
| Development or local databases | Cloud (with password) |
| Staging environments | Cloud (with password) |
| Production databases | Cloud (without password) |
| Environments with external secret storage policies | Cloud (without password) |
| Shared team machines | Cloud (without password) |
| Personal machine, trusted environment | Cloud (with password) |

A few rules of thumb:

- **Use Cloud (with password)** when you regularly switch between devices and need frictionless access to non-production databases.
- **Use Cloud (without password)** for any production database, or whenever your company's security policy requires that passwords not be stored outside your direct control.
- You can use different sync modes for different connections — for example, **Cloud (with password)** for your local dev database and **Cloud (without password)** for your staging and production connections.

## Open Source

Conar is open source and released under the **AGPL-3.0 license**. This means the encryption implementation — and every other part of how Conar handles your credentials — is publicly auditable. You don't have to take our word for how your data is protected.

You can review the source code at any time:

[github.com/wannabespace/conar](https://github.com/wannabespace/conar)

If you find a security concern in the codebase, please open an issue or contact the maintainers directly through the repository.

<Warning>
  Never share your connection strings with others — even colleagues. A connection string contains everything needed to access your database. If a connection string has been exposed, rotate your database password immediately and update the connection in Conar.
</Warning>

<Note>
  When you run a query in Conar, your credentials and query results travel directly between your machine and your database. Conar's cloud infrastructure stores only encrypted connection metadata — it never handles your live query traffic.
</Note>
